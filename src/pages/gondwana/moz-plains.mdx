## ...and the role of Limpopia

![Animation may take a few seconds to load](./assets/MozPlain-2.gif)

* [Select another animation](../animations)

For decades, understanding the tectonic history off SE Africa has been stalled on the argument as to whether the Mozambique Plains are continental or oceanic. In fact, they are neither.  They do, however, lie between text-book examples of both: The **Kaapvaal craton** (Precambrian continental crust of the African shield) to their west and the **Africa-Antarctica Corridor** (AAC), ocean crust grown about a central mid-ocean ridge that has produced 6000 km of oceanic crust since the onset of Gondwana break-up (i.e. at an average speed of about 33 km/My) to their east [(see Animation K)](https://www.reeves.nl/gondwana/africa-antarctica). 

The Plains themselves are interpreted here as crust that was extended above the Bouvet plume head (one of the most active and long-lived- [(see Animation I)](https://www.reeves.nl/gondwana/large-igneous-provinces)) and to include a large thickness of magmatic material dating from the period 200-150 Ma (Early and Middle Jurassic). The change in direction of Antarctica-Africa spreading at about 155 Ma (Kimmeridgian) and the increasing distance of the Plains from the Bouvet plume head caused the active spreading ridge to jump to south of the Plains at about 150 Ma. 

Even the 'tight' Gondwana reconstruction with Antarctica partially overlapping the Plains [(see Animation B)](https://www.reeves.nl/gondwana/grav.marg) leaves a lozenge-shaped gap, somewhat smaller than Sri Lanka, that we fill with the southernmost part of the present-day Mozambique Ridge.  This has the correct size and shape to fill the gap and is a clear feature on satellite altimetry of ocean floor bathymetry. It may well have a core of Precambrian rock originating in the area of the present-day Limpopo estuary and we have called it **Limpopia** [(Reeves 2017)](https://pubs.geoscienceworld.org/gsl/pg/article-abstract/24/1/41/524220/The-development-of-the-East-African-margin-during). It is one of several fragments that complicate the separation of East and West Gondwana (diagonal hachuring in the animation).

The **Biera High** originated in the Zambezi estuary and followed Antarctica at first before being left behind on the Africa plate at about the time of the 155 Ma change in spreading direction.

The **Grunehogna** remains to this day part of the Antarctica plate but shows evidence of rifting around its periphery in Mesozoic times (Pencksokket).

Intermediate between these two fragments, **Limpopia** remained part of the Antarctica plate until early in Cretaceous time when the active mid-ocean ridge in the AAC found a new path to its south where it joined the active spreading ridge in the Weddell Sea. By about 125 Ma (Barremian) Limpopia had become isolated at the edge of the Africa plate.  The newly active transform to its east, intially a leaky transform 135-130 Ma approximately, produced magma to build much of the present-day Mozambique Ridge at this time

A part of the Mozambique Plains created by 150 Ma broke off the Africa plate to travel independently as the **Natal Valley** fragment for a while before it, too, became distant from the plume head and became part of Africa again.

Post-155 Ma N-S movement necessitated a long-offset (1070 km) N-S dextral transform parallel to the Lebombo (green highlight). The Lebombo is part of a much longer fracture zone separating Antarctica from southernmost South America further south.  This became an active ridge as early as 165 Ma, extending NW to separate the Malvinas Plateau from the Maurice Ewing Bank (MEB) and bringing marine waters to the Outeniqua basins of South Africa. 

By about 124 Ma Barremian) the fragments off SE Africa had all stabilised onto the Africa plate, the MEB was becoming fixed to the South America plate as ocean-floor sprading between it and the Malvinas plateau dies out.  A 
ridge-ridge-ridge triple junction was established off SW Limpopia and proved to be long-lived.  By this time Antarctica was pursuing a new, arcuate path towards the SW while the mid-ocean ridge between it and Africa was still well northeast of Limpopia.  This created the observed curvature on the Africa side of the AAC that is absent from the Antarctic side.

A consequence of the model is that onshore southern Mozambique – the Mozambique Plains – is predicted to consist of extended continental crust hosting a failed ridge system and a copious supply of Early Jurassic magma.  The offshore crust, south of Maputo, meanwhile, is predominantly oceanic, post-dating 150 Ma (Kimmeridgian).  Remarkable is the absence of magmatic activity into the AAC east of the Mozambique Ridge. Ocean growth in the AAC proceeded steadily about a central mid-ocean ridge throughout the period under discussion.
